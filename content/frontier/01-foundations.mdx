> **A nation is the people. A polity is a people organized politically. A state is the enduring political structure. A government operates it. A country is the everyday word for the territorial whole.**

These words are often used interchangeably in ordinary conversation, but they do not mean the same thing. That distinction matters because network states, Settlemints, sovereignty, autonomy, jurisdiction, and recognition cannot be understood clearly until the basic political vocabulary is separated.

## The concise answer

- **Nation:** a people who understand themselves as a people.
- **Polity:** a community organized under a system of governance.
- **State:** an enduring political structure exercising governing authority.
- **Government:** the institutions and people currently making and carrying out public decisions.
- **Country:** the common-language term for the territorial and political whole.
- **Nation-state:** a state whose political structure and national people substantially coincide.

The terms overlap, but each points to a different part of political life.

## Nation

> **A nation is a people who understand themselves as a people.**

A nation is not primarily a government, border, or legal structure. It begins with a shared sense of **we**.

That shared identity may grow from:

- history,
- culture,
- language,
- religion,
- ancestry,
- covenant,
- memory,
- political identity,
- or a shared sense of destiny.

Not every nation has its own state. A nation may exist without controlling territory, possessing diplomatic recognition, or operating a sovereign government.

A social audience, token-holder base, online community, or large following does not automatically constitute a nation. The deeper question is:

> **Do these people meaningfully understand themselves as a people?**

## Polity

> **A polity is a people or community organized under some form of governance.**

This is the broadest useful political term in this chapter. A polity may be a city, tribe, kingdom, republic, self-governing community, or state.

Its importance for this guide is that it gives us a way to describe a politically organized community without prematurely claiming statehood.

A **network polity** can therefore mean a digitally coordinated community with meaningful membership, governance, institutions, collective action, and possibly physical embodiment—even if it has not become a state.

The useful progression is:

> **A nation is a people. A polity is a people organized politically. A state is a polity with state-level authority and standing.**

The final threshold remains contested and requires more precision later in the guide.

## State

> **A state is an enduring political structure that exercises governing authority over a population and territory.**

The exact definition varies across political theory and international law, but serious definitions usually involve:

- an enduring institutional structure,
- rules and public authority,
- a population,
- territory,
- the capacity to make and enforce decisions,
- and some degree of independence and external standing.

The state is not identical to the government currently in office.

Governments change. The state may persist.

This is why an administration can be replaced without the country ceasing to exist, and why a government can collapse while institutions of state continue.

### Sovereign states and constituent states

The word **state** creates additional confusion because it is used for both sovereign countries and subnational political units.

For example, the United States is a sovereign state in international affairs, while Texas is also called a state within the American federal system. Texas has substantial constitutional authority, but it is not independently sovereign in the same sense as the United States.

Later chapters must distinguish:

- sovereign state,
- constituent state,
- province,
- autonomous region,
- municipality,
- special jurisdiction,
- and other delegated or subnational authorities.

## Government

> **Government is the machinery through which political decisions are made and carried out.**

A government may include:

- executives,
- legislatures,
- courts,
- ministries,
- agencies,
- councils,
- enforcement bodies,
- and administrative systems.

A government may be legitimate or illegitimate, strong or weak, effective or symbolic. It may control the state fully or only partially.

A DAO vote, council, constitution, or governance platform is not automatically a functioning government.

The practical tests are whether:

- authority is structured,
- decisions can be made,
- decisions can be executed,
- disputes can be resolved,
- leaders can be held accountable,
- and the system continues beyond one personality or moment.

## Country

> **Country is the everyday word for the territorial and political whole.**

It is less technically precise than **state**, but often more natural in ordinary speech.

France may be described as a country, a state, and a nation-state. Each description emphasizes something different:

- **country** emphasizes the whole place and political community,
- **state** emphasizes the governing political structure,
- **nation-state** emphasizes the relationship between the national people and the state.

Use **country** when speaking plainly. Use **state** when political or legal precision matters.

## Nation-state

> **A nation-state is a state in which a national people and a sovereign political structure substantially coincide.**

This does not mean every resident shares one ancestry, culture, or identity. Nor does it mean every nation-state is culturally uniform.

The term describes the political ideal or condition in which a state understands itself as the political home of a particular nation.

Many real countries are multinational, contain minority nations, or include communities with competing national identities. The relationship between nation and state is therefore often incomplete or contested.

## The distinctions that matter

| Term | Plain meaning | What it indicates | What it does not prove |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Nation** | A people who understand themselves as a people | Shared identity and peoplehood | Government, territory, or sovereignty |
| **Polity** | A community organized politically | Governance and political organization | Statehood |
| **State** | An enduring political structure exercising governing authority | State-level political organization | Universal recognition or equal capacity in every case |
| **Government** | The institutions currently making and executing public decisions | An operating decision structure | Nationhood or sovereignty by itself |
| **Country** | The common word for the territorial-political whole | Everyday geographic and political identity | Precise legal status |
| **Nation-state** | A state substantially identified with a national people | Alignment between nation and state | Perfect cultural unity |

## Common category errors

### An audience is not automatically a nation

Large numbers, shared media, and online attention may create an audience. Nationhood requires a deeper and more durable sense of peoplehood.

### Governance software is not automatically a government

Voting tools, multisigs, constitutions, and smart contracts may support governance. They do not prove that legitimate authority exists or that decisions can be enforced.

### Land ownership is not automatically a state

Owning property creates legal rights within an existing jurisdiction. It does not ordinarily create independent political sovereignty over that land.

### A community can be a polity without being a state

This is one of the most useful distinctions for network-state discussions. Political organization may emerge before statehood.

### A state can contain several nations

The boundaries of political authority and national identity do not always align. One state may govern several peoples who retain distinct national identities.

## Why this matters for network states and Settlemints

The vocabulary lets us make more disciplined claims.

- A network community can become a **network polity** before becoming a **network state**.
- A Settlemint can host a people, community, or polity without itself being a state.
- A people may become a **nation** before they possess statehood.
- A network polity may develop governance without possessing sovereign authority.
- A project can look state-like long before it has proven the functions of a state.

This distinction prevents the guide from confusing identity, governance, territory, technology, and sovereignty.

## The sentence to remember

> **A nation is a people. A polity is a people organized politically. A state is the enduring political structure. A government runs it. A country is the everyday word for the whole.**
